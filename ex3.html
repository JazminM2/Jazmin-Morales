Abstract
¶

Given a table which contains the number of nights lost due to bad weather in an astronomical observatory, by months and years, the possible seasonal and long-term variation of the fraction of nights lost due to bad weather are analised. Such analysis is made through $chi^2$ statistics, in order to determine the goodness of fit for the models. When the fraction lost per year was taken into account, both the constant fit and the linear fit are consistent with data; in the other hand, for the fraction lost per month, the model that better describes the data involves a sinusoidal function.

Introduction¶

The analysis in this exercise is made under the following assumptions:

  • The different months and years are independent statistical samples.

  • Gaussianity in data and statistical errors

In order to treat the information given, the fractionf of nights lost due to bad weather is calculated for each year and for each month, resulting in sequence tuples:

$$\{ x_i, y_i, \sigma_i \} $$

Where $x_i$ is the year or month, $y_i$ is the fraction lost and $\sigma_i$ is the statistical error.

Given a model $f(\theta_1, ...,\theta_p)$ for the data, the goodnes of the fit is described by $\chi^2$ statistics , which is defined as (1):

$$\chi^2 (\theta_1, ..., \theta_p)= \sum_i^N \left( \frac{y_i - f(\theta_1, ..., \theta_p)}{\sigma_i} \right)^2 $$

$\chi^2$ tests if the data observed $y_i$ is 'close enough' to $f$ to have occurred given the supposition that

Three kinds of models are applied to observe the behaviour of the data: constant, linear and a curve.

  • Linear fit $y(x; a, b)= a + bx$:

The analytical solution $\chi^2$ is given by:

$$ \chi ^2 (x) = \sum^N_i \left( \frac{y_i-a-bx_i}{\sigma_i} \right) ^2 $$

With parameters $a,b$ (its determination is necessary for the characterisation of the model).

  • Constant fit $y(x;a) = a$:

It is similar to linear fit for the case $b=0$. Then $\chi^2$ is :

$$ \chi ^2 (x) = \sum^N_i \left( \frac{y_i-a}{\sigma_i} \right) ^2 $$
  • Curve $f(x; f_c, f_e, t_0 ) = fc + fe\times \sin(2\pi(x-t_0)/12)$:

For the three parameters $f_c, f_e, t_0$, $\chi^2 $ is given by :

$$ \chi^2 (f_c, f_e, t_0) = \sum^N_i \left( \frac{y_i-f(x;f_c, f_e, t_0 )}{\sigma_i} \right) ^2 $$

The goodnes of fit $Q$ of a $\chi^2$ fit is determined by:

$$ Q(\chi^2, \nu) = 1-P(\chi^2, \nu)$$

Where $P(\chi^2, \nu) = \text{Probability that} \chi^2 < \chi^2_{min}$ if the model is correct

If $Q=1-P << $, the model is not consistent with the data.

If $P << $, the model may correspond with the data.

$\nu$ is the number of degrees of freedom (the difference of independent data $N$ and the number of fitted parameters $p$):

$$\nu = N-p $$

Once the models are given, it is possible to know if the use of a model is better than the other; $F$ statistics allows to do it:

$$ F = \left( \frac{\chi_1^2-\chi_2^2}{\nu_1-\nu_2} \right) / \left( \frac{\chi_2^2}{\nu_2} \right) $$

When a model 2 improves on model 1, the probability $P$ is low.

The confidence intervals are the regions in the parameter space with high probability that the real value of such parameters is the value computed. This value depends on the number of parameters used:

  • The formula to use in lm for a constant, $y = cte.$, is:
$$y \sim 1$$

Errors can be taken into account in the fit: $$\text{weight} = 1/\sigma_i $$

with $\sigma_i$ the error in data1.

  • The analytical expression for $\chi^2$ when fitting to a constant, $y = a$ is :
$$ \chi ^2 (x) = \sum^N_i \left( \frac{y_i-a}{\sigma_i} \right) ^2 $$

The minimum $a_0$ is:

$$ a_0 = \sum^N_i \frac{y_i}{\sigma_i^2}/\sum \frac{1}{\sigma_i^2} $$

a3) Calculate 90% confidence interval.

For 1-parameter, the value of $\Delta \chi^2 $ with enclosed probability of 90% is: $ \Delta \chi^2 = 2.71$ (Blake, C.)

Where $$\Delta \chi^2 =\sum_i^N \frac{N(a-\bar{y})^2}{\sigma_i^2}$$

$$\Rightarrow a = \bar{y} \pm \sqrt{\Delta \chi^2/ N}\sigma $$

With $\bar{y} = a_0$

a_conf <- function(a0, N, sigma){
    a_left = sum(a0 - (sqrt(2.71 / N) * sigma))
    a_right = sum(a0 + (sqrt(2.71 / N) * sigma))
    return(c(a_left, a_right))
}

c3) Confidence intervals 90% and 99%

For linear fit, the confidence interval is given by $b_\delta$:

$$ b_\delta = b_0 \pm \sigma_0\sqrt{\delta} $$

Where:

$$\sigma_0 = \sqrt{1/(S_{xx}-S_x^2/S)} $$

$ S =\sum^N_i \frac{1}{\sigma^2_i} $,

$ S_x =\sum^N_i \frac{x_i}{\sigma^2_i} $,

$ S_{xx} =\sum^N_i \frac{x_i^2}{\sigma^2_i} $

The F statistics is defined as:

$ F = \left( \frac{\chi_1^2-\chi_2^2}{\nu_1-\nu_2} \right) / \left( \frac{\chi_2^2}{\nu_2} \right) $

Where $\chi_1^2$, $\nu_1$ stands for the constant fit; $\chi^2_2$, $\nu_2$ is the $\nu_2$ stands for the linear fit.

The improvement for models 1,2 is $\text{improve} = 1 - \text{pf}$

These results show that:

  • a) Assuming that the fraction of lost nights does not vary year-to-year, the value of such constant is a=-8.949. Its 90% confidence interval is (4,0762-- 5,2320)
  • b) The goodness of fit for a constant model is Q = 0,03, which means the model may be consistent with data.

$f(x;a,b)=a+bx$, with parameters a=-8.949, b=0.0045

  • d) From $F=0.00001$, the probability of improvement for constant fit and linear fit is low. Both models are consistent with data.

Conclusions¶

After the analysis of data with different models it can be concluded that the variation of lost nights over the years 1984-2005 behaves linearly, although it is also possible to describe the behaviour as constant. In the case of seasonal variation, the fraction of lost nights for months can be seen like sinusoidal variations.

Knowing the goodness of a fit is very important since a model with a wrong description of the behaviour of a set of data is used can led to a misinterpretation of such data. $\chi^2$ is a method which allows to make a detailed analysis of data.
